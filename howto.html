How to Cut Crown Molding

The Ultimate Step-by-Step Guide

Cutting crown molding is notoriously tricky because the wood sits at an angle against the wall and ceiling. To get a perfect fit, you need to master a few pro secrets.

1. The Golden Rule: "Upside Down and Backwards"

Most DIYers fail because they try to cut the molding as it sits on the wall. Instead, follow these steps on your miter saw:

  • Upside Down: Place the ceiling edge of the molding flat on the saw table.
  • Backwards: The wall edge of the molding should rest against the saw fence.
Pro Tip: Think of the saw table as your ceiling and the saw fence as your wall.

2. Setting Your Angles

If you aren't using a "crown stop" fence, you need to set both the Miter Angle and the Bevel Angle.

Getting these numbers right is the hardest part. Instead of guessing, use our Crown Molding Angle Calculator to get the exact settings for your specific corner and spring angle.

3. Understanding Spring Angles

The "Spring Angle" is the angle at which the molding leans away from the wall.

  • 38¬∞ Spring Angle: Most common for standard molding.
  • 45¬∞ Spring Angle: Common for larger, decorative molding.
  • 52¬∞ Spring Angle: Often used in high-end or custom builds.
Unsure which one you have? Lay a scrap piece in a framing square; the distance it reaches on the wall vs. the ceiling will reveal your angle.

4. Inside vs. Outside Corners

  • Inside Corners: These are the corners where two walls meet (like the corner of a room).
  • Outside Corners: These occur where a wall turns a corner (like a chimney breast or a hallway turn).

5. Why Precision Matters

Even a 0.5-degree error in your wall angle can leave a massive gap in your trim. We recommend using a digital angle finder to measure your actual wall corner before cutting.
